History of Computing in Medicine

Diagnostics

Computers have been used in medicine for much longer than you might think. In the early days of computing development, it was mainly used in medicine to more efficiently conduct patient surveys.

Slack's first patient history program, which he had used in early 1965, contained 450 screening questions. Each question had four possible responses. "Yes", "No", "Don't know", and "Don't understand". ...Each question was printed on a punched data processing card. After the patient had sorted Slack's cards by placing each card into an appropriate slot of a divided tray, the sorted cards were fed into an automatic punched card reader that transmitted the responses to a computer, which then generated a summary printout of the patient's responses.

The History of Medical Informatics in the United States, ISBN 978-1-4471-6731-0

Later in the same year, Slack and his colleagues stopped using punched cards for this and used a more sophisticated system with a screen and an interface, but this very early use of computation in diagnostics demonstrates how far back the link between the two fields goes.

MUMPS

MUMPS, or Massachusetts General Hospital Utility Multi- Programming System, is a programming language that was designed in 1966 for use in healthcare. It is designed for use with databases, and can handle high throughput. This proved very useful in the 60s for tracking patient records and other information efficiently, and MUMPS is still used today by many healthcare IT companies and in the financial sector.

Robotic Surgery

By the early 2000s, some forms of robotic-assisted surgery were being used regularly to shorten the length of the surgery, decrease the amount of bleeding, and improve a number of other factors. These types of procedures are still carried out by human surgeons, but the use of robotics to detect the movement of the surgeon and carry it out on the patient has many advantages, such as the ability of the robotic system to filter out hand tremors, and much less time being lost due to surgeons not having to scrub in and out for shifts during long surgeries.
